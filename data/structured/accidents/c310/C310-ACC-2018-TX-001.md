# Prevention Leverage

A standardized departure briefing requiring verification of single-engine climb performance at current weight and density altitude, combined with a defined engine-failure decision gate below a specified altitude, may have altered the outcome.

# Narrative Summary

The twin-engine aircraft departed a high-elevation airport during daytime visual meteorological conditions. Shortly after liftoff and during initial climb, the left engine experienced a partial loss of power. Witnesses reported the aircraft yawing and rolling before descending.

The investigation identified no preimpact mechanical anomalies with flight controls. Postaccident engine examination confirmed a reduction in power on the left engine; however, the engine was capable of producing some power at impact. Performance calculations conducted during the investigation indicated that, at the aircraft’s weight and prevailing density altitude, single-engine climb performance would have been marginal.

The pilot had accumulated less than 50 hours in the Cessna 310 model. The aircraft entered a descending roll and impacted terrain beyond the runway environment. The aircraft was destroyed by impact forces and post-impact fire. The passenger survived with minor injuries; the pilot sustained fatal injuries.

The investigation cited inadequate airspeed control following the loss of engine power and the pilot’s inability to maintain control during the initial climb phase.
