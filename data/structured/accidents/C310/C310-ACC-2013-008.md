# Narrative Summary

The pilot filed an IFR flight plan for a night flight to Jacksonville and declined a weather briefing even though the destination was reporting low IFR conditions. During the ILS approach, the airplane passed the final approach fix about 1 mile right of course and about 900 feet below the published crossing altitude.

The tower controller issued a low-altitude alert, and the pilot twice crossed left of the final approach course before intercepting it again. After descending to about 300 feet, the pilot reported that he would conduct a missed approach.

Instead of following the published missed approach climb profile, the airplane entered a climbing left turn and reached about 900 feet before radar and radio contact were lost. It then descended into a pond about 1 mile south of the airport.

Examination of the airframe, engines, and flight instruments found no preimpact mechanical malfunction that would have prevented normal operation. Both engines were later successfully test-run.

The investigation found that the pilot’s inability to meet the approach’s lateral and vertical constraints was consistent with lack of instrument proficiency. The NTSB concluded that the pilot lost control during the missed approach in night IMC due to spatial disorientation and insufficient instrument proficiency.
