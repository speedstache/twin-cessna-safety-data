# Narrative Summary

A Cessna 310C on an introductory instructional flight and a Piper PA-28 were both maneuvering in a busy local practice area at 6,500 feet mean sea level when they collided in midair. The Cessna returned to the departure airport and landed uneventfully with substantial damage to the vertical stabilizer and rudder.

The instructor reported seeing an approaching aircraft only moments before impact. The pilot of the Piper later made a forced landing after collision damage compromised the fuel system and led to engine power loss.

The local FAA inspector reported that the practice area was widely used for training and that pilots normally monitored the Jean Airport CTAF while operating there. None of the pilots reported using that CTAF, and instead they were monitoring only the departure airport tower frequency.

The pilot receiving instruction in the Cessna had about 1,600 hours total time but only 6 hours in make and model. The flight instructor had 11,349 hours total time and 32 hours in make and model.

Although visual lookout failures are central to the accident narrative, the available approved factor taxonomy does not contain a precise see-and-avoid category, so the contributing factor was coded as unknown.
