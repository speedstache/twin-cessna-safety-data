# Narrative Summary

A witness observed the airplane depart from the uncontrolled airport, but the accident itself was not seen. The wreckage was discovered two days later in wooded terrain about 1 mile from the departure end of the runway and about three-quarters of a mile to the right of extended centerline.

The impact attitude was nearly inverted, and the location of the wreckage was inconsistent with the normal left traffic pattern for the departure runway. Investigators concluded that these features were consistent with a loss of control roll to the right after takeoff.

The right fuel boost pump switch was found in the on position while the left boost pump switch was off. Although switch movement during impact could not be ruled out, the as-found condition was consistent with an attempted response to a loss of right engine power.

Examination of the airframe, engines, propellers, and carburetors did not reveal any preimpact mechanical malfunction that would have prevented normal operation. Both engines ran normally during postaccident testing.

The pilot had extensive total flight time and about 500 hours in the make and model, but the last documented flight review and medical evidence available to the investigation dated to 2007. The NTSB determined that the pilot failed to maintain control after a loss of right engine power for undetermined reasons.
