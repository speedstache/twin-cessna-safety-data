# Narrative Summary

The noninstrument-rated pilot, a pilot-rated passenger, and a passenger were attempting to return from the Bahamas to Florida while a weather front was passing over the state. The pilot-rated passenger obtained multiple weather briefings that described low ceilings, poor visibility, rain, and thunderstorms, and a briefer advised that VFR flight was not recommended.

Despite those advisories, the crew continued the trip and later discussed trying to “scud run” home. The pilot’s wife also reported that the destination weather was bad and believed they would wait until the next day.

About 20 minutes after departing Sebastian, the pilot-rated passenger contacted Orlando Approach and reported that the flight had inadvertently entered instrument meteorological conditions. Radar data then showed the airplane descending in multiple circuits from 8,900 feet msl to very low altitude before the final target was recorded near the accident site.

The wreckage was found the next day in a densely wooded swamp, largely intact and without a debris path. Given the radar track, marginal weather, and the pilot’s lack of an instrument rating, investigators concluded that the pilot likely experienced spatial disorientation.

Toxicology found diphenhydramine in the pilot’s blood at levels within or above the therapeutic range. The NTSB concluded that continuation of VFR flight into IMC, impaired judgment, and pressure to get home contributed to the accident.
