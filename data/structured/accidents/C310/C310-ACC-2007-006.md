# Narrative Summary

On October 7, 2007, a Cessna 310N, N85WT, collided with terrain approximately 15 miles south of Ekalaka, Montana, during an IFR cross-country flight conducted under Part 91. The instrument-rated private pilot and one passenger were fatally injured. About 40 minutes after departure at a cruise altitude of 13,000 feet msl, the pilot reported encountering icing conditions and requested lower altitudes. Despite multiple descents, he continued to report icing and subsequently transmitted that he was “headed towards the runway” and “going down…troubles with engine.” No further communications were received. Wreckage signatures were consistent with a stall/mush into terrain during descent. Postaccident examination revealed no evidence of preimpact mechanical malfunction. The airplane was not certified or equipped for flight into known icing conditions, and weather products and AIRMETs along the route forecast moderate icing. The NTSB determined the probable cause was the pilot’s improper in-flight planning and continued flight into icing conditions, and failure to maintain adequate airspeed during the emergency descent.
