# Narrative Summary

On May 5, 2016, at 1331 central daylight time, a Cessna 310F twin-engine airplane, N6770X, impacted terrain following a loss of control during approach for landing at North Little Rock Municipal Airport in North Little Rock, Arkansas. The designated pilot examiner was fatally injured and the commercial pilot sustained serious injuries. The airplane was destroyed by postimpact fire.

The pilot was conducting an airline transport pilot checkride under Part 91 in day VFR conditions. During initial climb on the upwind leg at approximately 700 feet msl, the left engine experienced a partial loss of power to 2,200 rpm. The pilot and examiner agreed to keep the engine running because it was still providing "positive thrust." The airplane climbed to 800 feet msl and the pilot elected to make left turns in the traffic pattern.

On the downwind leg, the left engine lost additional power and the pilot attempted to feather the left propeller by moving the control full aft; however, the propeller did not feather. Radar data indicated continuous airspeed loss as the airplane turned from downwind toward the airport. The aircraft collided with terrain in a 45-degree left bank at low altitude and was subsequently destroyed by postcrash fire.

Post-accident examination revealed no mechanical malfunction preventing normal engine operation. The left propeller remained in the windmilling position, not feathered, with blades at or near start lock position at impact. It is likely the pilot allowed engine rpm to decay below the 800 rpm start lock engagement threshold, preventing feathering and resulting in significant parasitic drag and loss of single-engine climb performance.
