# Narrative Summary

On June 8, 2013, a Cessna 340A impacted shallow water near Boynton Beach, Florida, and was destroyed; the pilot was fatally injured. About four minutes after takeoff on an IFR flight, the pilot advised departure control that the airplane was having "instrument problems" and requested to remain VFR for the climb. Controllers accommodated the request and coordinated between sectors; the pilot later resumed climbing and continued under IFR. Miami Center advised of moderate-to-heavy precipitation along the route and approved deviations; the pilot did not advise the center controller about the instrument issue until later. Radar data showed the airplane turned toward heavier precipitation, climbed to about 9,500 feet, then rapidly descended to near ground level before recovering to about 1,500 feet. The airplane likely stalled and descended into the waters of a wildlife refuge after climbing back into instrument meteorological conditions. Wreckage evidence indicated the engines were producing power at impact, and flight control surfaces were accounted for at the scene despite extensive fragmentation. The investigation could not determine which instruments failed or the extent of redundant-system failures.
