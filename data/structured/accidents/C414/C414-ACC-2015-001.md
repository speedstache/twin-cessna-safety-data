# Narrative Summary

On April 7, 2015, a Cessna 414A was conducting an IFR Part 91 personal flight to Central Illinois Regional Airport at night.

The destination weather about one minute before the accident included a 200-foot overcast ceiling and one-half mile visibility in light rain and fog.

The airplane was vectored for the ILS runway 20 approach.

Radar and electronic horizontal situation indicator data showed that the airplane did not properly intercept or track the localizer or glideslope.

The airplane crossed the final approach fix below glideslope and remained right of the localizer centerline.

The airplane turned left away from the localizer before reaching the missed approach point and did not follow the published missed approach instructions.

The glideslope antenna cable was found inadequately connected or secured, resulting in no usable glideslope indication to the cockpit avionics.

Weight-and-balance calculations showed the airplane was over maximum landing weight and aft of the allowable center-of-gravity limit at the time of the accident.

The NTSB determined that the pilot failed to maintain control during the instrument approach, with fatigue, increased workload from lack of glideslope guidance, and aft loading contributing.
