# Narrative Summary

The Cessna 414 departed Bedford County Airport on a Part 91 personal IFR flight. The pilot estimated that visibility was greater than one-half mile at departure. During initial climb, about one to two miles from the runway, the airplane collided with trees while in instrument meteorological conditions. The airplane continued climbing and shortly afterward exited IMC. The pilot observed damage to both wing leading edges and fuel leakage, declared an emergency, and diverted to a nearby airport. During the subsequent landing, the pilot received an unsafe nose gear indication and landed on the main landing gear before the propeller contacted the runway. The closest weather observation reported low visibility, 100 feet vertical visibility, and fog near the time of departure. The published obstacle departure procedure required a 300-foot-per-nautical-mile climb gradient, and the pilot reported no preflight aircraft discrepancies. The NTSB attributed the accident to failure to establish a proper climb gradient during an IMC departure, resulting in a collision with trees.
